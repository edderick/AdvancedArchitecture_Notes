Flip-flops

From Sotonpedia
Jump to: navigation, search

Flip flops are areas of circuitry that are able to store 1 bit of data. This means they can serve as an area of memory.
In modern parlence a flip-flop has come to mean a non-transparent device that uses a clock line and can be used to hold one bit on its output line, whereas the simpler transparent latch doesn't need a clock line (and it therefore simpler but more difficult to implement into things like pipelines and delay lines). Dennis (Denis?) will probably use the terms flip-flop, latch and D-type interchangeably as he's Dennis (or Denis).

[edit] Latches

File:Sr-latch.png
NAND gate Latch

The most common latch uses two NAND gates.
This diagram has two inputs, set (S) and reset (R), and an output (Q) (Â¬Q is just the inverse of the output). Pulling S high will result in the output (Q) being set to 1 and staying at this value, no matter if S stays high or goes low again. Pulling R high will set Q to 0, and Q will stay at this value no matter if R stays high or goes low again. This important property of keeping a value stored in the circuit is the basis for memory. Also notice how this is volatile, as in if the power is switched off all the values are lost.



[edit] Flip-Flops

Flip-flops perform in much the same way as a Latch does as they essentially use a Latch at their heart, but they contain a few important differences because of the surrounding circuitry. The most notable are the differing inputs, as instead of set and reset it uses Data (D) and Clock (Clk).
The most common flip-flop (or at least the only one Dennis seems to care about) is the D-type flip-flop.

[edit] D-type

File:D-type flip-flop.png
D-Type Flip-Flop

The D-Type flip-flop has the same outputs as the Latch, so it has the same ability to store a bit at the Latch does. The diagram still shows the S and R inputs on the chip, as these are the actual Set and Reset lines exposed from the inner latch. However these are very rarely used, and are normally ignored on any circuit diagram. The important lines are D and Clk. D-types are edge-triggered which means that when the Clk line changes from one state to another the circuit will start performing. (It depends on how the internals of the circuit work for whether a rising or falling edge will trigger it). When the flip-flop recieves an edge on Clk it will take the current value of the line at D and copy it to the output Q. As long as Clk doesn't recieve another edge the output Q will stay at the same value, no matter what D is changed to. This introduces the concept of clocking and storing data, which is a central concept to pipelining.
